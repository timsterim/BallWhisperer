Our Philosophy

Your Sherpa

The ball has lots of stuff going on. It has velocity, spin, trajectory, and a rebound coefficient (how bouncy the ball and paddle are together) that all have a huge effect on the outcome of your ball strike. Navigating all of this to produce good shots is essentially what experience will deliver to all of us. But for those who don't want to wait for that deliverance, getting quality guidance will get you there faster.

A Typical Session

Physics, Kinesiology, Balance and Presence

Sessions start with a gratitude infused evocation. Most instruction focuses on understanding the 'why' behind all aspects of the game which informs our actions and intentions. Concepts like preparation, loading and executing are easier to grasp when you know the why. With the greater understanding, the sessions become live ball drill, often where I am the ball machine putting your ball back in the exact position to apply what we are working on. I put significant effort in modeling the behavior I want my students to exhibit, like split stepping and chasing every ball like your life depends on it.

An Experience vs a lesson

The Neurology of Presence and Gratitude

Eckhart Tolle has suggested that sports deliver a substantial and effective meditative practice. So that autopilot that is executing without you thinking is what presence is all about. You aren't feeling guilty when chasing or hitting a ball nor do you stress about anything other than what is happening at the moment. Some use a lotus position, we chase balls. Mindsets like gratitude enhance brain function. Balance gives the cerebellum extra resources to smack your shot. Balance + Presence = a sweet shot = joy. It's just science!

"Before and after every point or ball in play, be centered, breathe and be grateful that you are playing a game."

Tennis has a combative quality unlike almost any other sport. Ancient parts of the brain light up — the same circuits wired for survival — and in doing so, the game hands us a rare opportunity: to confront the ego directly, point by point, rally by rally. The Zen tradition speaks of "dying before you die," surrendering attachment to outcome so that presence can fill the space. Tennis offers exactly that classroom, whether we show up as a student or not.

The challenge, of course, is detachment. We are asked to care deeply about winning while holding the result loosely — to play with full commitment and zero clinging. This is perhaps the most difficult thing a human being can attempt. It is also the most rewarding. As the Bhagavad Gita puts it: act without attachment to the fruits of the action. Every point is the entire practice.

The fitness dimension is almost a side effect. When your ego is on the line chasing a ball down, the fatigue barely registers. Tennis delivers genuine cardiovascular conditioning, a flood of endorphins, and — according to several longitudinal studies — a life expectancy roughly ten years beyond the average. Free exercise has never been so engrossing.

And then there is the sweet spot. Physically speaking, it is the node where the racket's natural vibration frequency cancels itself out — meaning virtually zero energy is lost to the frame on impact, and nearly all of it transfers directly into the ball. The forces at work are remarkable: rotational momentum stored in the swing, the spring-like rebound of the strings, and the lever mechanics of a 27-inch frame all converge in a region about the size of a silver dollar. Hit it, and the racket feels like it weighs nothing. The ball simply leaves — fast, clean, and deep — with an almost embarrassing lack of effort. That contrast between minimal input and outsized output is the mechanical magic of the sweet spot, and the brain knows it immediately. Dopamine floods in. The body files it away and quietly starts scheming to make it happen again. This is why tennis players keep coming back — not just to win, but to feel that again.

Pickleball is far more accessible than tennis, and uniquely friendly to players of different skill levels — which is exactly why it's the fastest growing sport in America, and it isn't close.

Here are the vital skills you will develop working with Tim:

  • 01
    Basic Footwork

    No skill simplifies the game more than footwork. Players who skip it compensate by swinging harder — which is far less repeatable than using the feet and torso to set up every shot.

  • 02
    The Dink is Your Friend

    Ironically the most accessible shot for beginners, yet a genuine challenge for tennis players conditioned to drive through the ball. The dink has the smallest margin of error in the game — which is exactly why learning its technique pays off so quickly and so well.

  • 03
    The Drive

    Many players take the long and winding road with their backswing — a habit that is deeply counter-productive and quietly ruins footwork at the same time. We address this early, and the results tend to be immediate.

  • 04
    The Serve

    A high-leverage skill you can practice entirely on your own. Learn to mix velocity, trajectory, and spin to keep opponents perpetually off balance — before the rally even begins.

  • 05
    Preparation with Paddle

    Second only to footwork in its impact on swing mechanics. Getting the paddle ready early is one of those adjustments that delivers visible improvement almost immediately — and it tends to surprise people.

  • 06
    Topspin is a Must

    Topspin allows you to swing more aggressively without the ball sailing out — a freedom that proves transformative for most students. Once you have it, you won't remember how you played without it.

  • 07
    Specialty Shots

    The offensive topspin lob, the defensive lob, the touch drop shot, the Around the Post — these shots change what's possible on the court. You'll learn not just how to hit them, but when to attempt them and why.

  • 08
    The Inner Game of Pickleball

    The most neglected dimension of most practice routines. Learning to manage the mind, emotions, ego, and even the cardiovascular system pays enormous dividends — and requires far less practice than people expect.

Golf is the most humbling game ever invented — and also the most rewarding. Unlike racket sports where reaction time carries you, golf is entirely premeditated: every shot is a deliberate act of intention, physics, and feel. The good news is that deliberate can be coached, measured, and transformed.

All golf sessions are held at BetterGolf in the Capitola Mall, where a Uneekor launch monitor running GSPro captures your swing with two high-speed cameras at up to 500 frames per second. Every session generates a complete data portrait of your ball and club — club path, face angle, attack angle, smash factor, spin axis, carry distance, and dozens more — all analyzed in real time by AI. There is nowhere for a flaw to hide, and nowhere for a breakthrough to go unnoticed. You will see your improvement as it happens.

Here is the curriculum Tim works through with every golfer:

  • 01
    Grip

    The only point of contact between you and the club — and the single most overlooked fundamental in the game. A faulty grip quietly corrupts everything downstream: face angle, wrist hinge, release, and impact. We establish a neutral, pressure-aware grip early, and the launch monitor confirms it instantly through face-to-path data. Get this right and the rest of the game becomes learnable.

  • 02
    Stance & Setup

    Posture, ball position, alignment, and weight distribution are the architecture the swing is built on. The Uneekor cameras measure attack angle and dynamic loft at impact — metrics that expose setup errors invisible to the naked eye. Small adjustments here create leverage gains that no amount of swing work can replicate. As with all ball sports, preparation is everything.

  • 03
    The Full Swing

    The golf swing is a kinetic chain: ground force travels through the feet, hips rotate and lead, the torso follows, the arms are passengers, and the club is the caboose. When each link fires in sequence, the result is effortless power — the golf equivalent of a tennis sweet spot. GSPro's smash factor metric (ball speed ÷ club speed) tells us precisely how efficiently energy is being transferred. We chase that number, and the yards take care of themselves.

  • 04
    Chipping & the Short Game

    Roughly 60% of all golf shots happen within 100 yards of the hole. Most amateurs spend 90% of their practice time ignoring this. We flip that equation. You'll learn a reliable chip technique built on consistent contact, controlled spin, and predictable landing zones — and the simulator's spin rate and launch angle data makes the feedback instantaneous and unambiguous.

  • 05
    Putting

    Putting is a game within the game — one where feel, rhythm, and green-reading matter more than power. We work on stroke path, face angle at contact, and tempo, developing a repeatable motion that holds up under pressure. The simulator allows us to play real courses so you develop touch across different green speeds and slopes, not just a flat practice mat.

  • 06
    Trouble Shots

    Bunkers, tight lies, uneven stances, punching under branches, playing from the rough — these are the shots that separate scorers from sufferers. Each demands a specific adjustment to setup and swing mechanics, and each can be drilled in the simulator before you ever face them on the course. Confidence in trouble is a skill. It is teachable.

  • 07
    Working the Ball

    The ability to intentionally shape shots — a controlled draw around a dogleg, a high fade into a tight pin — is where golf becomes truly artistic. The Uneekor captures spin axis in real time, which makes learning to curve the ball a concrete, feedback-rich process rather than guesswork. The data shows exactly which swing adjustments produce which ball flights. This is the part where the game starts to feel like a conversation.

  • 08
    Course Management

    The smartest shot is rarely the most aggressive one. GSPro lets us play full 18-hole rounds on world-class courses in the simulator, which creates real strategic decisions in real time: club selection, target lines, risk/reward on the par 5, playing away from the trouble on the right. Good course management is pattern recognition — and patterns develop faster when you play 18 holes in 90 minutes.

  • 09
    The Mental Game

    Golf is the sport most ruthlessly governed by the mind. There is simply too much time between shots for an undisciplined mind to stay out of its own way. We work on pre-shot routines that anchor attention in the present, breathing techniques that lower heart rate and cortisol before high-stakes shots, and the art of forgetting the last hole — which, as any Zen teacher will tell you, is the same art as living well. Balance, presence, execution: the same three words that govern every sport we teach apply here with full force. The simulator removes the noise of the outdoor environment and lets us practice mental protocols in a focused, distraction-free space — so when you're standing over a critical putt on a real course, the calm isn't something you hope for. It's something you've rehearsed.
